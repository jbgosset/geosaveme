## How to Use This Catalogue

112 call-takers answer and process emergency calls that may relate to an acute illness or injury, traffic accidents, crimes, incidents that threaten law and order, fires, or social emergency situations.

This catalogue describes **ten major security situation categories** that PSAP operators must be trained to handle. For each category you will find:

- **Situation description** — what the call looks like, key variants
- **Priority questions** — what to ask and in what order
- **Pre-arrival instructions** — what to tell the caller while help is en route
- **Dispatch decision guidance** — which ERO to engage
- **Common pitfalls** — errors to avoid
- **Good practices** — evidence-based and EENA-recommended approaches

## Situation 1 — Cardiac Arrest / Respiratory Emergency

### 1.1 Description

Cardiac arrest is the highest-priority medical situation a call-taker will face. Every second without CPR reduces survival probability by approximately 10%. The most studied emergency for pre-arrival instructions is sudden cardiac arrest. When cardiac arrest occurs in a community setting, fewer than half of victims receive bystander chest compressions.

**Typical call presentations:**
- "My husband collapsed and isn't breathing"
- "Someone fell down in the street, they're unconscious"
- "They're breathing strangely / making gurgling sounds" *(likely agonal breathing)*

### 1.2 Key Challenge — Agonal Breathing

The most difficult factor that can confound the pre-arrival instructor is identifying agonal respirations — irregular, gasping breaths that may be mistaken for normal breathing. Any abnormal breathing pattern in an unresponsive patient should be treated as cardiac arrest.

### 1.3 Priority Questions

```
CALL-TAKER SCRIPT — CARDIAC ARREST RECOGNITION:

Step 1 — Confirm location (dispatch runs simultaneously)
Step 2 — "Is the person conscious?" → if NO or unsure →
Step 3 — "Are they breathing normally?" → if NO or gasping →
Step 4 — ACTIVATE CARDIAC ARREST PROTOCOL ("no-no-go" rule)
Step 5 — Ask: "Is there an AED nearby?" (guide to use if available)
Step 6 — Begin Telephone-CPR (T-CPR) instructions
```

### 1.4 Telephone-CPR (T-CPR) Protocol — Standard Instructions

Dispatch-Assisted CPR (D-CPR) is now considered the standard of care.

```
1. "Call for help / shout for help in the area"
2. "Put the phone on speaker and place it next to the person"
3. "Lay the person flat on their back on a hard surface"
4. "Place the heel of one hand in the centre of the chest"
5. "Put your other hand on top and interlock your fingers"
6. "Press down firmly and quickly — I will count with you"
   → Count aloud: "1-2-3-4-5..." (100–120 compressions/minute)
7. "Do not stop unless I tell you, or help arrives and takes over"
8. "Keep pushing until help takes over — you are doing well"
```

> **Compressions-only CPR is the current standard** for adult sudden collapse. Rescue breaths are optional and should only be instructed if the caller has PSE/BLS training. Do not delay compressions for airway management.

Implementation of assertive step-by-step T-CPR instructions has been shown to produce a 41% relative increase in the rate of telephone-CPR and improved survival from cardiac arrest.

### 1.5 Dispatch

- **Immediate ECHO-level response** (highest priority)
- EMS + SMUR (if French system) / Advanced Life Support unit
- Notify nearest AED location to caller if available
- Keep line open throughout

### 1.6 Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Correct Approach |
|---------|-----------------|
| Accepting "yes, breathing" without probing | Ask: "Is the breathing normal? Not gasping or noisy?" |
| Delaying dispatch to complete questioning | Dispatch immediately — questions continue in parallel |
| Instructing mouth-to-mouth first | Start compressions immediately; rescue breaths are secondary |
| Hanging up after dispatch | Stay on line; guide and reassure until EMS confirmed on scene |
| Mistaking agonal breathing for normal | Any irregular, noisy, or gasping breath = treat as arrest |

## Situation 2 — Acute Medical Illness (Non-Arrest)

### 2.1 Description

Covers a wide spectrum: suspected stroke, seizure, hypoglycaemia, severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis), asthma attack, chest pain, loss of consciousness with spontaneous breathing, intoxication.

**Common call presentations:**
- "She's having a seizure / shaking all over"
- "He's complaining of chest pain and sweating"
- "His face looks droopy and he can't speak properly"
- "They've taken too many tablets / pills"

### 2.2 Priority Questions by Condition

**Suspected Stroke (FAST check via caller):**
```
F — "Can they smile? Is one side of the face drooping?"
A — "Can they lift both arms and hold them up?"
S — "Can they speak clearly, or is their speech slurred?"
T — Time of onset: "When did this start?" → CRITICAL for thrombolysis window
```

**Seizure:**
```
→ "Are they still convulsing or has it stopped?"
→ "Are they breathing?" / "Are there any injuries visible?"
→ "How long has this been going on?"
→ Instructions: do NOT restrain; protect head; clear area; time the episode
→ If >5 minutes duration: escalate to ECHO-level
```

**Anaphylaxis:**
```
→ "Do they have an EpiPen / auto-injector?" → instruct use immediately
→ "Are they conscious and breathing?" / "Any swelling of face, lips, throat?"
→ Lay flat, legs elevated (unless breathing difficulty)
→ Dispatch ALS/SMUR
```

**Hypoglycaemia (conscious patient):**
```
→ "Are they conscious and able to swallow?"
→ If yes: instruct 15–20g fast-acting sugar (juice, sugar, glucose gel)
→ If no: do NOT give anything by mouth; treat as unconscious
```

### 2.3 Pre-Arrival Instructions

Pre-arrival instructions may include general safety, medication, providing access for responders, and hemorrhage control.

- Keep person calm; do not leave them alone
- Loosen tight clothing (collar, belt)
- Do not give food/drink unless explicitly instructed (hypoglycaemia exception)
- PLS (recovery position) for unconscious breathing patient
- Unlock door / send someone to meet responders

### 2.4 Dispatch

- Stroke, MI, anaphylaxis, prolonged seizure → SMUR/ALS dispatch
- Alert receiving hospital for time-critical conditions (stroke, MI)
- PSAPs are often responsible for monitoring the status of local hospitals, advising paramedics on which hospitals are accepting ambulance patients and which are on divert.

## Situation 3 — Road Traffic Accident (RTA / AVP)

### 3.1 Description

Traffic accidents generate some of the highest call volumes and the most complex multi-agency dispatch decisions. A traffic accident on a major highway may result in multiple 112 calls reporting the same location.

**Key variants:**
- Single vehicle, single occupant (possibly eCall-triggered)
- Multi-vehicle, multiple casualties
- Pedestrian or cyclist struck
- Hazardous cargo vehicle involved
- Motorway incident with secondary accident risk

### 3.2 Priority Questions

```
SCENE ASSESSMENT — RTA PROTOCOL:

1. LOCATION: Precise road, direction, motorway km marker, junction number
2. VEHICLES: How many vehicles involved? Type (car, truck, motorbike)?
3. VICTIMS: How many people? Are any trapped? Any ejected?
4. CONSCIOUSNESS: Are people conscious and responsive?
5. HAZARDS:
   → Fuel spill? Fire? Smoke?
   → Oncoming traffic hazard?
   → Dangerous goods vehicle? (Look for orange/black HAZCHEM panels)
   → Electric vehicle? (High-voltage risk for rescue crews)
6. eCALL: Has an eCall already been triggered? (Check screen for MSD data)
```

### 3.3 Pre-Arrival Instructions (Caller at Scene)

```
Safety first:
→ "If it is safe to do so, move away from the vehicle(s) — 50m minimum"
→ "Switch on hazard lights on your vehicle if you are stopped"
→ "Do NOT smoke or use a lighter near the vehicles"
→ "If you see fuel leaking, keep away from the area"

Victims:
→ "Do NOT move anyone unless there is immediate danger (fire)"
→ "If conscious, reassure them and keep them still"
→ "If not breathing: begin CPR if it is safe and you are able to"
→ "Apply firm pressure to any severe bleeding with a cloth"
```

> **Key rule:** Do NOT instruct caller to move injured persons — spinal injury risk. Only authorise immediate movement if vehicle is on fire or caller's life is at risk.

### 3.4 Multi-Agency Dispatch

| Situation | Primary ERO | Secondary |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Injuries + no fire | SAMU (15) + SDIS (18) | Police (17) for traffic control |
| Fire or fuel hazard | SDIS (18) first | SAMU (15), Police (17) |
| Trapped persons | SDIS (18) with désincarcération | SAMU (15) |
| HAZCHEM vehicle | SDIS (18) NRBC team | SAMU (15), Gendarmerie |
| Motorway / autoroute | All + ASF/SAPN (motorway authority) | |

### 3.5 eCall-Specific Handling at RTA

- If eCall received: MSD provides location, direction, trigger type
- Automatic trigger = vehicle sensors detected high-severity crash → dispatch immediately even if no voice contact
- Check fuel type in MSD (electric vehicles require specialist approach; HV cable risk)
- Standard EN 15722:2020 mandates provision of the two most recent vehicle locations before an incident, crucial for PSAPs to furnish accurate information to emergency services — particularly on motorways or bridges.

## Situation 4 — Fire & Explosion

### 4.1 Description

Fire calls span a wide priority spectrum — from alarm activations (often false) to life-threatening structural fires. The top five fire call protocols in structured dispatch systems are: Alarms, Outside Fire, Structure Fire, Citizen Assist/Service Call, and Electrical Hazard.

**Variants:**
- Residential structure fire (with or without persons inside)
- Commercial / industrial fire
- Vehicle fire
- Outdoor/forest fire
- Gas leak / explosion
- Electrical incident
- Alarm activation (high false-alarm rate)

### 4.2 Priority Questions

```
FIRE CALL ASSESSMENT:

1. LOCATION: Full address, floor/level, access route
2. NATURE: "What is on fire?" — structure, vehicle, outdoor, unknown
3. PERSONS:
   → "Are there people inside the building?"
   → "Is anyone trapped or unable to evacuate?"
   → "Are there any injuries?" / "Is anyone burned?"
4. FIRE STATUS:
   → "Can you see flames? Smoke? From where?"
   → "Is the fire spreading?"
5. HAZARDS:
   → Gas cylinders? Chemical storage? Industrial equipment?
   → Lift shaft? Underground parking?
6. ACCESS: "What is the main entrance? Is the door open?"
```

### 4.3 Priority Levels (FPDS Model)

The highest priority level, ECHO, represents the most time-critical cases that require an immediate response by the absolutely closest available and capable responder, and lifesaving pre-arrival instructions provided over the phone.

| Priority | Trigger | Response |
|----------|---------|----------|
| **ECHO** | Person trapped, burns, structural collapse imminent | Multi-unit HOT response + T-PAI |
| **DELTA** | Active structure fire, spreading | Multi-unit HOT response |
| **CHARLIE** | Fire visible, unknown if spreading | Multi-unit, HOT or COLD per SOP |
| **BRAVO** | Smoke visible, no flames confirmed | Single unit HOT |
| **ALPHA** | Alarm activation, no visible fire | Single unit COLD |

### 4.4 Pre-Arrival Instructions

**If persons are inside an affected building:**
```
→ "Leave the building immediately by the nearest staircase — do NOT use lifts"
→ "Close all doors behind you as you leave"
→ "If smoke in corridor: stay low and crawl below the smoke level"
→ "If you cannot escape: close the door, seal gaps with clothing, go to a window"
→ "Signal from the window — I will relay your position to firefighters"
→ "Do NOT open the door if the handle is hot — fire is on the other side"
```

**For outdoor observers:**
```
→ "Stay back from the building — at least 100 metres"
→ "Do not re-enter under any circumstances"
→ "Keep the road clear for fire vehicles"
```

If someone is trapped in a burning building, PSAP operators can advise them on ways to stay safe until firefighters arrive.

### 4.5 Dispatch

- All structure fires with persons: SDIS (18) + SAMU (15)
- Gas leak without fire: SDIS (18) + utility company notification
- Industrial fire with HAZCHEM: SDIS NRBC + SAMU + police perimeter
- Forest fire: SDIS + Sécurité civile aerial resources if large scale

## Situation 5 — Crime in Progress (Theft, Robbery, Burglary)

### 5.1 Description

Crime calls require PSAP operators to simultaneously support a frightened or endangered caller, extract actionable intelligence for police, and assess escalation risk — all without placing the caller in greater danger.

**Variants:**
- Robbery with weapon in progress (aggravated)
- Residential burglary (in progress vs. discovered afterwards)
- Street theft / bag snatch
- Carjacking
- Shoplifting with violence
- Cybercrime (unusual PSAP involvement; usually referred)

### 5.2 Priority Questions

```
CRIME IN PROGRESS PROTOCOL:

1. LOCATION: Exact address / street / business name
2. NATURE: "What is happening right now?"
3. SUSPECT:
   → "How many people are involved?"
   → "Can you describe them? Clothing, height, direction of travel?"
   → "Do you see any weapons?"
4. VICTIM STATUS: "Are you or anyone else injured?"
5. CALLER SAFETY: "Where are you? Are you safe?"
6. SUSPECT STATUS: "Have they left? Which direction? On foot or vehicle?"
   → If vehicle: "Colour, make, registration plate?"
```

> **Priority rule:** Do NOT ask callers to follow suspects. Instruct them to stay safe, stay on line, and observe from a distance only.

### 5.3 Pre-Arrival Instructions

```
→ "Stay where you are and keep yourself safe"
→ "Do NOT confront or approach the suspect(s)"
→ "If possible, move to a safe location within the building"
→ "Keep watch from a safe distance and tell me if anything changes"
→ "Do not touch anything at the scene — preserve evidence"
→ "Help is on the way — stay on the line with me"
```

### 5.4 Dispatch

- Police (17) / Gendarmerie — priority level based on weapon/injury information
- SAMU (15) if injuries reported
- Escalate to firearms-trained units if weapon confirmed

## Situation 6 — Harassment, Aggression & Domestic Violence

### 6.1 Description

Domestic violence calls are among the most frequent and most dangerous situations for both victims and responding officers. The caller may be unable to speak freely, may minimise the severity, or may suddenly retract the report.

**Variants:**
- Physical assault in progress (domestic partner, family member)
- Threat with weapon (domestic)
- Street harassment / aggressive pursuit
- Stalking (ongoing threat)
- Sexual assault

### 6.2 The Silent / Covert Caller — Special Protocol

Many domestic violence callers cannot speak freely because the aggressor is present. If unsafe for the caller to speak freely, instruct the caller to put the phone down and keep the line open.

```
COVERT CALL PROTOCOL:
→ Speak slowly: "If you can hear me but cannot speak, press a key"
→ Confirm: "Press 1 for yes, 2 for no"
→ "Are you in danger right now?" → [key response]
→ "Do you need police?" → [key response]
→ Dispatch immediately; do not wait for verbal confirmation
→ Keep line open; AML/location data is your location source
→ Relay to units: "Caller unable to speak — silent call — possible DV"
```

### 6.3 Priority Questions (When Caller Can Speak)

```
1. "Where are you?" (confirm location — may differ from registered address)
2. "Are you hurt?"
3. "Where is the person now? Same room / building / left?"
4. "Do they have a weapon?"
5. "Are children present?"
6. "Has this happened before? Have police been called before?"
7. "Is there somewhere safe you can go right now?"
```

### 6.4 Call-Taker Good Practices for DV

Call takers and dispatchers should gather as much information as possible at the time of the call. Ideally, the call taker is still on the line as units arrive, providing near-real-time intelligence on what is likely to be a situation in flux.

- **Never** express scepticism or minimise the caller's account
- **Never** ask "Why don't you just leave?" — demonstrates misunderstanding of DV dynamics
- Check prior call history to the same address in CAD
- Flag for follow-up: many DV situations escalate after the first police response
- Ensure caller knows about **3114** (France) or national DV helpline as a secondary resource

### 6.5 Dispatch

- Police (17) with DV-specific briefing (weapon? children? prior history?)
- SAMU (15) if injuries
- Children present → alert child protection services (social emergency pathway)

### 6.6 France-Specific — 3919 & 114

| Resource | Number | Use |
|----------|--------|-----|
| Violences Femmes Info | **3919** | DV hotline, 24/7, free |
| Emergency (deaf/HoH) | **114** | Silent emergency via SMS/video |
| Social emergency | **115** | Homeless, persons in danger |
| Child protection | **119** | Child abuse, maltreatment |

## Situation 7 — Mental Health Crisis & Suicide Risk

### 7.1 Description

Mental health-related calls are among the most psychologically demanding for call-takers. They require de-escalation skill, structured risk assessment, and knowledge of specialist referral pathways.

**Variants:**
- Active suicidal ideation (caller or third party)
- Suicide attempt in progress
- Psychiatric decompensation / acute psychosis
- Drug/alcohol crisis with behavioural disturbance
- Welfare check (concerned caller about a third party)

### 7.2 Active Suicidal Crisis — Call-Taker Protocol

```
APPROACH:
→ Use a calm, non-judgmental, unhurried tone
→ Do not argue with or challenge the person's feelings
→ Use their name if known
→ Acknowledge: "I can hear that you're in a lot of pain right now"

RISK QUESTIONS (asked gently, not as a checklist):
→ "Are you thinking about ending your life?"
→ "Do you have a plan / method in mind?"
→ "Have you taken anything or hurt yourself already?"
→ "Where are you right now?"

KEEP THE LINE OPEN:
→ Do not hang up; even silence is a connection
→ Dispatch SAMU (15) or police in parallel — do not wait for consent
→ If method in place: escalate immediately (ECHO-equivalent)
```

> **France note:** The national suicide prevention line **3114** (staffed by psychiatric nurses) can be conferenced in to support complex calls. Activate CUMP for mass casualty psychological trauma events.

### 7.3 Welfare Check (Third-Party Concern)

```
→ Get caller's relationship to the person
→ Last known contact / when was the person last seen?
→ Does the person have a known history of mental illness or prior attempts?
→ Dispatch police for welfare check; SAMU (15) on standby if risk is high
```

### 7.4 Good Practices

- Do not trivialise or dismiss the call, even if the caller seems calm
- Avoid yes/no questions; use open questions to maintain dialogue
- Medical dispatching includes call-taking, triage, dispatch, and providing callers gesture guidance to the victims. Every decision has a major impact on the patient's outcome.
- Log psychological context in CAD for responding units
- Provide debriefing support to the call-taker after the call

## Situation 8 — Public Order, Threat & Terrorism

### 8.1 Description

Public order emergencies range from crowd disorder to active threats and terrorist incidents. These calls demand simultaneous multi-agency coordination and call-surge management.

**Variants:**
- Fight / brawl in public place
- Armed individual / active threat
- Terrorist attack (mass shooting, vehicle ramming, bombing)
- Protest turning violent
- Hostage situation
- Suspicious package

### 8.2 Active Threat / Terrorist Incident Protocol

```
IMMEDIATE STEPS:
1. Confirm location — as precise as possible (building name, entrance, floor)
2. "How many people are involved / how many attackers?"
3. "Do you see weapons? What type?"
4. "Are there injuries / casualties visible?"
5. "Where are you? Are you in a safe location?"

DISPATCH: Police (RAID/BRI if France) immediately — do not wait
INFORM: Escalate to supervisor / activate mass casualty plan
MANAGE CALL SURGE: Flag as multi-caller incident in CAD
```

**Caller safety instructions (active threat):**
```
RUN: "If there is a safe exit route, leave immediately and go as far as possible"
HIDE: "If you cannot run: hide, lock or barricade the door, silence your phone"
FIGHT: [Only as absolute last resort — not typically instructed by call-taker]
→ "I am staying with you. Help is on the way. Keep the line open."
```

> **French plan VIGIPIRATE:** Call-takers should be briefed on current threat level and aware of protocol escalations under Plan Rouge and Plan Terroriste.

### 8.3 Call Overload During Mass Events

In incidents such as terrorist attacks, some calls may add valuable information — additional descriptions of suspects, number of people affected, better descriptions of the scene.

Best practices during call surge:
- Prioritise new-information callers; fast-close duplicate-location calls
- Activate overflow PSAP links and neighbouring centre support
- Broadcast a recorded message to non-connected callers where legally permitted
- Log all calls even if no operator available — for post-incident analysis

## Situation 9 — Social Emergency

### 9.1 Description

Social emergencies are situations where a person is in danger due to social, not purely medical or criminal, circumstances. They are often routed to 112 because callers don't know where else to turn.

**Variants:**
- Homelessness / person sleeping rough in extreme weather
- Abandoned or lost child
- Elderly person found confused / wandering (dementia)
- Person in extreme distress due to poverty or isolation
- Death notification (caller discovers a body)
- Newborn or child emergency (accidental / unexpected)

### 9.2 Key Resources (French System)

| Situation | First Contact | Secondary |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| Homeless in danger | **115** (SAMU Social) | Police (17) if safety issue |
| Lost / missing child | **116 000** (EU missing child) | Police (17) |
| Child maltreatment | **119** (protection enfance) | Police (17) |
| Elderly / dementia | SAMU (15) if medical risk | Police (17) welfare check |
| Body discovered | Police (17) | SAMU (15) if life signs unclear |
| Unexpected childbirth | SAMU (15) / 15 → SMUR | SDIS (18) if very urgent |

### 9.3 Unexpected Childbirth — Pre-Arrival Instructions

This is a specific scenario in the PSE1 curriculum and relevant to PSAP pre-arrival guidance:

```
1. "Help is on the way — stay calm"
2. "Ask the mother to lie down somewhere clean and comfortable"
3. "Do NOT try to stop or slow down the delivery"
4. "If the baby comes before help arrives: support the baby gently — do not pull"
5. "Wrap the baby in a clean towel or clothing immediately"
6. "Do not cut the cord — lay the baby on the mother's stomach"
7. "Keep mother and baby warm"
8. "Tell me immediately if the baby is not crying or breathing"
```

### 9.4 Discovery of a Body

```
→ "Can you see any signs of breathing?"
→ "Is there any visible injury or sign of how long they have been there?"
→ Dispatch SAMU (15) first — determination of death is medical
→ Police (17) if circumstances are suspicious
→ Instruct caller NOT to touch or move anything
→ "I know this is very difficult. Help is on the way."
```

## Situation 10 — False, Nuisance & Ambiguous Calls

### 10.1 Classification

Calls received at PSAPs can be categorised as: real emergency calls (imminent threats to life, property or environment), and false emergency calls (all other calls).

False calls are sub-classified as:
- **Intentional adult** — deliberate harassment
- **Youth/child play** — unintentional or exploratory
- **Technical false** — misdial, pocket dial, SIM-less call
- **Good faith incorrect** — caller believed there was an emergency (alarm malfunction, misidentification)
- **Consciously non-responsive** — caller stays silent to tie up line

### 10.2 Protocol for Suspected False Calls

```
→ NEVER abandon a call that might be genuine
→ Attempt voice contact: "112, can you hear me?"
→ If silent: attempt key-press confirmation (1 for yes, 2 for no)
→ Use AML/CLI data to confirm or follow up location
→ If no response after protocol steps: dispatch welfare check
→ Log call: number, time, AML location, outcome
→ Only blacklist after confirmed pattern and legal authority to do so
```

> **Rule:** Always treat a silent call as potentially genuine. A domestic violence victim, a person in shock, or a child who dialled accidentally may be unable to speak.

### 10.3 Reducing False Call Impact

- Callback protocol: use standard callback number (0 800 112 112 in France)
- CAD history check: flag repeat callers
- Liaise with network operator for SIM-less call patterns
- The use of call handling processes that include standard questions to filter false emergency calls, combined with call-back capability, represents EENA best practice.

## Cross-Situation Good Practices Summary

### Universal Call-Taker Standards

Protocols serve as the foundation of call-taking and dispatching, providing a method for structured information gathering that allows call-takers to obtain the information they need in a logical and consistent manner. Protocols reduce dispatcher stress — they don't have to come up with the questions on their own.

| Practice | Applies To | Why |
|----------|------------|-----|
| **Dispatch while questioning** | ALL situations | Zero-minute response time from call answer |
| **Open question first, closed second** | Medical, DV, crisis | Gets richer information faster |
| **Keep line open** | Cardiac arrest, active threat, silent caller | Situation is dynamic |
| **Name the caller** | Mental health, DV, frightened callers | Anchors connection, reduces panic |
| **Confirm location at least twice** | ALL | Most frequent source of dispatch error |
| **Pre-arrival instructions** | Medical, fire, RTA, cardiac arrest | Makes caller an active participant |
| **CAD entry in real time** | ALL | Ensures team situational awareness |
| **Supervisor escalation trigger** | Mass casualty, terrorism, child at risk | Activates additional resources |
| **Post-call self-assessment** | Traumatic calls | Early stress recognition, debrief request |

### Caller Communication Principles

```
DO:
✔ Speak clearly and calmly
✔ Use simple language — no jargon
✔ Acknowledge the caller's emotion before gathering information
✔ Reassure: "Help is on the way"
✔ Give one instruction at a time
✔ Tell the caller what to expect next

DON'T:
✘ Ask multiple questions at once
✘ Say "calm down" — say "I am with you"
✘ Use passive voice ("it might be that…") when clarity is needed
✘ Speculate on outcome or diagnosis
✘ Suggest delay or minimise urgency
✘ Hang up before units are confirmed on scene (in life-threat calls)
```
