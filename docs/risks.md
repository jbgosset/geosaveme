# Usage Risks & Mitigations

## Risk Register

### 1. False Alerts — Strategic Diversion
**Risk:** False alerts deliberately emitted to draw security forces away from a planned aggression.

**Mitigations:**
- Is this materially worse than existing false alerts? Probably not for the Rescue level. However, Vigilance and Alert levels create a new perimeter of solicitation that does not currently exist.
- The application geolocates the call, making it impossible to misrepresent the location.
- After the fact, intervention forces can qualify a hotspot as a "false alarm", thereby discrediting the issuer.

---

### 2. Counter-Aggression — Aggressor Becomes Aware of the Alert
**Risk:** The aggressor notices the alert being raised, triggering an escalated attack.

**Mitigations:**
- Alerts are broadcast to the network (excluding first responders and professionals) with a deliberate degree of geographic blur to minimise the risk of identifying the issuer.
- Ghost mode allows the alert to be sent exclusively to security professionals, bypassing local broadcast entirely.

---

### 3. Aggressor Aware of Incoming Forces
**Risk:** The aggressor becomes aware of approaching security forces and flees.

**Mitigation:**
- Security forces can operate in stealth mode, remaining invisible on the map to prevent the aggressor from anticipating their arrival.

---

### 4. Anxiety Effect
**Risk:** Awareness of local insecurity levels increases anxiety and stress among users.

*No mitigation currently proposed. This is an inherent tension of any transparency-based safety platform.*

---

### 5. Informant / Tattletale Network Effect
**Risk:** The platform encourages a culture of reporting, releasing repressed aggression in frustrated individuals.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 6. Victimisation Network Effect
**Risk:** The platform amplifies and normalises a culture of victimhood.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 7. Surveillance Anxiety ("1984 Effect")
**Risk:** Users fear participating in a generalised surveillance system. This could undermine public trust and limit adoption if the platform were perceived as being turned against its users.

*No mitigation currently proposed — though the private-sector structure of the vehicle is a relevant safeguard (see commercialisation notes).*

---

### 8. Over-Reaction / Snowball Effect
**Risk:** A situation that would have defused naturally instead escalates due to alert broadcast generating a snowball effect.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 9. Abuse — Frivolous Alerts
**Risk:** Users treat the platform as a game, generating significant noise and undermining alert credibility.

*No mitigation currently proposed. The credibility scoring system provides partial protection.*

---

### 10. Alert Overload — Revealed Reality
**Risk:** The platform surfaces a continuous stream of incivilities and alerts, exposing an underestimated level of daily violence. This makes it harder for security forces to prioritise effectively.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 11. Mass Events — Saturation
**Risk:** Violent demonstrations saturate the application; the network becomes unreadable.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 12. Vigilante / Private Militia
**Risk:** Organised groups use the platform to coordinate vigilante activity.

*No mitigation currently proposed.*

---

### 13. Institutional Resistance — Liability Concerns
**Risk:** Security forces resist integration because they do not wish to be held accountable for failing to act on information they were provably aware of ("you knew about it, you should have responded").

**Mitigations:**
- Precedent: online pre-filing already exists for property offences but not personal assault — illustrating the existing institutional boundary.
- Official forces cannot reasonably be held responsible for managing the continuous information flow that this type of platform generates.
- The platform can act upstream as a proxy — qualifying and helping to prioritise urgent situations while reducing direct calls to the emergency number (17) through local prevention. This liability concern is reduced if the platform structure is officially private.

> This risk merits deeper reflection. The platform's role as a pre-triage and prioritisation layer — rather than a direct reporting obligation — may be the key framing argument for institutional partners.
